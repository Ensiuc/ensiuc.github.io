I am a researcher driven by a deep fascination with how the environment interacts with the genome and epigenome to shape biological outcomes across generations. My early research explored how ecological factors and captivity influence genome-wide variation and epigenetic modifications in natural populations. During my PhD, I used population genomics and epigenomic profiling to study the molecular signatures of environmental stress and domestication, particularly in conservation-relevant fish species. That work deepened my appreciation for how plastic and dynamic the genome can be in response to external cues, and it taught me the value of integrating field-based questions with high-resolution molecular tools.

Building on that foundation, in my postdoctoral work, I focus on how **preconception experiences**—such as stress or environmental exposures—affect DNA methylation in oocytes. I use single-cell methylome sequencing to profile CpG and CpH methylation across individual oocytes and embryos, identifying how maternal environments alter the epigenetic landscape prior to fertilization. My analysis includes clustering cells by epigenetic state, mapping differentially methylated regions and genes, and integrating chromatin state annotations to understand the functional impact of these changes. I am particularly interested in how disrupted or resilient methylation patterns influence early embryonic development and long-term outcomes.

I deeply love the research I do. It gives me the opportunity to ask meaningful questions about biology, work with powerful technologies, and contribute to fields that matter—from fertility science to environmental epigenetics. My long-term goal is to lead a research program that bridges basic epigenetic mechanisms with translational outcomes in reproductive health, developmental biology, and precision medicine.
